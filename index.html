Legacies of the Code

An animated overview of how Goa and Puducherry's colonial civil codes survived Indian integration and continue to shape inheritance today.

I

Goa's Portuguese Code

The Portuguese Civil Code of 1867 applies uniformly to all Goans regardless of religion — a unique regime of matrimonial communion of property that survived Indian integration.

II

Forced Heirship (Legítima)

The 'legítima' reserves half the estate strictly for biological descendants — a parent cannot fully disinherit their children, however estranged.

III

Puducherry's Renonçants

Since 1881, certain Puducherry citizens renounced personal law to be governed by the French Civil Code — a lineage that persists across three generations today.

05 — Case Hypothetical

The Case of the Distinct Inheritors

In The Supreme Court of India, 2029 — four authored judicial opinions in the Speluncean Explorers style.

Twin references — one from the Bombay HC (Goa Bench) and one from the Madras HC — are consolidated before this bench on a single question of inheritance law.

The Uniform Civil Code Act, 2029, passed by the Central government, threatens to displace the distinct systems of Goa and Puducherry. The Portuguese Civil Code, preserved by Article 372 of the Constitution, faced extinction. The Puducherry legislature had separately revived French civil law traditions through the Puducherry Hindu Succession (Repeal) Act, 2028.

This court had previously stayed the UCC's extension to these territories by interim order. The question now before the bench is:

Whether the doctrines of forced heirship and matrimonial communion of property constitute a distinct yet coherent system of inheritance law — whose community-rooted conception of the family as an economic unit is so fundamentally different from the individual-testator model of the UCC — as to be incapable of substitution without legislative deliberation on why one model is preferred over the other.
I

Kunsh Jain, C.J.

The Portuguese Civil Code owes its continued existence to Article 372, which preserved all existing laws until Parliament legislated otherwise. The question before us is which model of family life the law ought to institutionalise — a question Parliament alone is equipped to answer.

I would refer the matter to a Joint Parliamentary Committee to examine the UCC's application to Goa and Puducherry. I express no opinion on the merits. There are moments when the highest form of judicial courage is the recognition of where judicial competence ends.

II

Sadhana Kasi, J.

It is ironic that the UCC now seeks to displace the very Goan system this Court praised in Jose Paulo Coutinho (2019). Under the Goan regime, all property becomes jointly owned upon marriage — a wife's written consent is required to accept or renounce an inheritance. Removing this is not a neutral act.

The 21st Law Commission held that discrimination, not difference, is the root of inequality. Forced heirship serves community interests by not leaving dependants desolate. Being incommensurable models, the UCC cannot wholly subsume this framework. The current one should prevail.

III

Animesh Jain, J.

What exists in Goa is a quasi-uniform regime. Article 1108 creates communion of assets, yet Article 1117 vests administration exclusively with the husband — rendering the wife's ownership a legal fiction. The Church retains influence over Catholic marriages; Gentile Hindus are permitted limited polygamy. Portugal itself has since amended these laws.

India's obligations under CEDAW cannot be met while these arrangements persist. Forced heirship further restricts testamentary freedom. The paradigm of family-as-collective-unit versus the UCC's autonomy cannot be easily reconciled — any subsumption would vitiate both.

IV

Manyata Singh, J.

The Goan system's apparent uniformity may in fact be uniformity in discrimination. Women routinely mistake the declaration of intent for formal marriage registration — finding themselves as unregistered wives with no claim to shared assets. Grounds of divorce differ by gender: a husband need prove only adultery; a wife must additionally prove public scandal or abandonment.

In Puducherry, descendants of the Renonçants are bound today by a choice made by their grandfathers in 1881 — a regime that has not evolved alongside contemporary developments. The UCC may validly be extended to both territories; the modalities are appropriately left to Parliament.

When there is a will, is there a way?

Exploring the distinct inheritance systems of Goa and Puducherry in modern India, where colonial civil codes outlasted empire and the question of uniformity meets the question of justice.

01 — Infographic Video

Legacies of the Code

An animated overview of how Goa and Puducherry's colonial civil codes survived Indian integration and continue to shape inheritance today.

I

Goa's Portuguese Code

The Portuguese Civil Code of 1867 applies uniformly to all Goans regardless of religion — a unique regime of matrimonial communion of property that survived Indian integration.

II

Forced Heirship (Legítima)

The 'legítima' reserves half the estate strictly for biological descendants — a parent cannot fully disinherit their children, however estranged.

III

Puducherry's Renonçants

Since 1881, certain Puducherry citizens renounced personal law to be governed by the French Civil Code — a lineage that persists across three generations today.

05 — Case Hypothetical

The Case of the Distinct Inheritors

A hypothetical case at the intersection of personal laws, inheritance, equality, gender, and the UCC — four authored judicial opinions, each member following the Speluncean Explorers style.

I

Kunsh Jain, C.J.

The question of which model of family life the law ought to institutionalise is one Parliament alone is equipped to answer. It requires not judicial inquiry but the democratic backing of a nation's considered judgment about how families ought to hold and share property. I would refer the matter back to Parliament for examination by a Joint Parliamentary Committee, with the interim stay continuing until such consideration. I am aware this may be seen as judicial abdication — but there are moments when the highest form of judicial courage is the recognition of where judicial competence ends.

II

Sadhana Kasi, J.

Removing matrimonial communion of property under the guise of a UCC is not a neutral act — it strips a specific economic protection crucial for the survival and autonomy of a significant section of society. The 21st Law Commission of India held in 2018 that discrimination, not difference, is the root of inequality: making everything uniform does not automatically eliminate inequality. Forced heirship serves community interests by not leaving dependants desolate. Being incommensurable models, the UCC cannot subsume this framework — the current one should prevail.

III

Animesh Jain, J.

The Portuguese Civil Code applies uniformly across religious communities, yet its allegiance to century-old provisions reveals persistent asymmetries — particularly for women. Article 1117 gives the husband exclusive administration of all marital property, rendering the wife's legal entitlement a fiction. Forced heirship, by mandating distribution to biological heirs, can prevent testators from providing for chosen families and non-traditional relationships. The paradigm of the Goan regime's family-as-collective-economic-unit versus the UCC's autonomy and equality of agency cannot be easily reconciled.

IV

Manyata Singh, J.

The apparent uniform character of the Goan system may be one of uniformity in discrimination. Bigamy remains permitted for certain Gentile Hindus; a wife must prove adultery plus public scandal for divorce while a husband need prove only adultery; complex marriage registration procedures leave many women as unregistered wives with no claim to shared assets. Continuing legal regimes rooted in colonial history results in a fragmented framework incompatible with Article 44's vision of national uniformity. The UCC may validly be extended to Goa and Puducherry — the modalities of implementation are appropriately left to Parliament.
